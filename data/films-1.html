This 1981 slasher horror was one of a slew of derivative movies (Friday the 13th, Prom Night, The Burning) that followed in the wake of John Carpenter's Halloween. Set in a small mining town during a Valentine's Day celebration, the film follows the tried-and-tested formula of horny, partying teenagers being stalked like lambs to the slaughter by a hack-happy boogeyman (in this case, a mad pickaxe-wielding miner). There's the odd creepy moment, particularly when the death-dealing moves to a genuine mine. But the bland characters and sub-par scares and gore (the movie was reputedly slashed by censors in the US and the version available in the UK seems shorn of full-on bloodletting) just go to show how good Carpenter's 1978 classic really is. Director: George Mihalka

This minor-league festive offering would have probably gone unnoticed had it not contained one of Patrick Swayze's final performances. Not that he's really the star attraction here, as it's the kids who take centre stage in a caper that relocates Miracle on 34th Street and Home Alone to a super-sized shopping mall. High jinks of an all-too predictable nature ensue when Swayze's jobless parent is tasked with carrying out the Christmas shopping with his three children in tow, leading to the discovery of a bag of counterfeit cash and a run in with three bumbling crooks and some overeager cops. The mild cynicism injected by bad guy Chris Kattan and middle sibling Matthew Knight offers some respite from the formulaic action, but Swayze is given little to work with as the bewildered dad, while Tim Curry's shameless turn as a Royal Mountie with an inexplicable Scot's accent is sheer panto excess.
